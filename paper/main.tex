\begin{abstract}
Internet of Things deployments fragment under cascading device failures and targeted jamming, and a network merely reconnected after a fault often remains one link from the next collapse. We argue that resilience needs a closed loop rather than a one shot repair, and present a self-healing framework that couples a lightweight AI perception layer, which detects and localizes degradation, with a quantum-inspired optimization layer, which recomputes a resilient configuration under an energy budget. The synergy is concrete: the perception layer emits a risk map that warm-starts and prunes the quantum-inspired search through its probabilistic qubit state, and recovered configurations retrain the detector. Casting repair as budget constrained survivability maximization, we evaluate the loop on two threats, random cascading failures and adversarial link jamming, over fifty scenarios each against genetic, simulated annealing, and greedy baselines. In both, myopic repair leaves the network fragile while the loop restores it; the AI warm-start makes healing more reliable and faster under cascades, and significantly better under jamming. We report honestly: the quantum-inspired optimizer is competitive, beating simulated annealing while trailing a tuned genetic search, and its value is being the natural substrate for fusing learned guidance into combinatorial healing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} Internet of Things now carries traffic that critical infrastructure depends on, yet the networks that carry it are fragile. Battery exhaustion, weather, and physical tampering remove nodes in bursts, and a single hub loss can cascade as newly isolated neighbours drop in turn. Adversaries add a sharper threat: a jammer can silence a band of channels at the very moment demand spikes. The shared property of these events is not that they disconnect a network once, but that they leave it brittle, sitting on single points of failure where the next small disturbance becomes the next outage.

Classical recovery tends to be reactive and myopic. A controller that reconnects the largest possible component, or that patches only the cheapest few broken links, optimizes the situation in front of it and stops. Such repair is fast, but it spends little thought on the failure after this one, so the healed network is connected without being survivable. Making a network survivable, meaning it stays connected after any further single failure, is a global and combinatorial property: it usually requires several coordinated link activations that close redundant cycles, where no single activation looks worthwhile on its own. This is precisely where a greedy controller stalls, a behaviour we both observe and dissect by ablation.

Two technologies are maturing that, together, address this gap. Artificial intelligence at the edge can detect and localize degradation in milliseconds from streaming telemetry. Quantum-inspired optimization brings population based, anytime, and highly parallel global search to the combinatorial reconfiguration problem, without requiring a quantum processor. Our thesis is that their synergy, not either one alone, enables genuine self-healing: the learned risk map is the right object to steer a global optimizer, and the optimizer is the right engine to turn that map into a resilient configuration.

We make four contributions. First, we formalize self-healing as a closed loop and cast repair as budget constrained survivability maximization. Second, we identify and instrument a concrete synergy mechanism: the perception layer warm-starts and prunes the quantum-inspired search through its qubit amplitudes, and recovered configurations retrain the detector. Third, we evaluate the loop on two distinct resilience threats with multi seed statistics. Fourth, we provide an ablation that isolates the warm-start, the search-pruning pull, the robustness weight, and the energy budget, and we report an honest portfolio comparison that locates the quantum-inspired optimizer's value in fusion and reliability rather than raw dominance.

\section{Background and Related Work}\label{sec:background}

\noindent\textbf{Resilience and survivability.} The classical networking literature distinguishes connectivity from survivability, the ability to keep operating through failures and attacks. Survivable network design, such as building two edge or two vertex connected topologies so that no single failure partitions the network, is a mature but largely offline and NP-hard discipline. Online restoration, by contrast, has tended toward fast reactive heuristics. Network science contributes complementary robustness measures, from percolation thresholds under random and targeted removal to spectral quantities such as algebraic connectivity and effective resistance. Our objective borrows from this tradition: we optimize a survivability measure rather than mere connectivity, and we use algebraic connectivity as a smooth robustness regularizer.

\noindent\textbf{AI for network management.} Learning based methods now pervade network operation, from traffic prediction and anomaly detection to reinforcement learning for routing and resource allocation. Their strength is fast inference from noisy telemetry and the ability to anticipate. Their weakness, for combinatorial repair, is that a learned policy does not by itself guarantee a globally feasible and resilient configuration under hard budget constraints. This motivates pairing learning with an explicit optimizer rather than replacing one with the other.

\noindent\textbf{Quantum and quantum-inspired optimization.} Gate model quantum optimization, including variational and approximate optimization algorithms, targets exactly the combinatorial problems that arise in network design, but current devices remain small and noisy. Quantum-inspired evolutionary algorithms capture part of the benefit on classical hardware: a probabilistic qubit encoding and rotation based update give a compact, population based, and naturally parallel global search. We adopt this quantum-inspired route deliberately, both because it scales to realistic IoT sizes today and because its probabilistic state is, as we show, the natural interface for injecting AI guidance.

\noindent\textbf{Quantum-AI for networks.} A fast growing body of work studies the intersection, including quantum federated learning over classical and quantum networks and in space air ground integrated systems, quantum machine learning as a primitive for communication intelligence, and quantum or quantum-inspired optimization for routing and allocation. Most of this work targets either learning or optimization in isolation, and treats faults as a backdrop. The gap we address is the closed loop between them, with resilience as the explicit objective: using learned perception to drive quantum-inspired combinatorial repair, and feeding the repair outcomes back to the perception layer, so the network keeps healing as it is damaged in real time.

\section{Why a Quantum-AI Synergy for Resilience}\label{sec:why}

Resilience in IoT spans several coupled challenges: topological robustness against node and link loss, robustness of wireless access against interference and jamming, and all of it under tight energy budgets. No single technique answers all of them, which is why a framework that combines complementary strengths is attractive.

Artificial intelligence contributes speed and anticipation. A small model running at a gateway or on a device can flag which nodes are becoming critical, predict where a cascade will propagate, and estimate post fault demand, all far faster than any exhaustive search. What it does not provide is a guarantee that its decisions compose into a globally resilient configuration.

Quantum-inspired optimization contributes global combinatorial search. Quantum-inspired evolutionary algorithms represent each decision variable as a qubit whose state is a probability, evolve a population of such probabilistic individuals, and rotate them toward the best solutions observed. They run on commodity hardware, scale to large IoT instances, are naturally parallel, and are anytime, returning a usable configuration whenever the deadline arrives. What they lack is any sense of where in a vast search space the good solutions lie.

The synergy follows from these complementary gaps, and it is more than co-location. The perception layer outputs exactly the prior that the optimizer needs, and the optimizer's probabilistic substrate is exactly the place to absorb it: a learned per element risk score maps directly onto the initial qubit amplitudes, warm-starting the population in promising regions and gently pruning the rest. A purely classical genetic algorithm has no equally natural slot for such a prior. \subsection{Notation and Fault Model}
We model the IoT deployment as an undirected graph $G=(V,E)$ with $n=|V|$ nodes. A fault event removes a node set $V_f\subseteq V$; under the targeted cascade we study, the highest degree node is removed repeatedly and any node left with no surviving neighbour is dropped, leaving the surviving set $V_a=V\setminus V_f$ and the residual graph $G_a=(V_a,E_a)$, where $E_a=\{(u,v)\in E : u,v\in V_a\}$. Let $\mathcal{C}=\{e_1,\dots,e_C\}$ be a pre surveyed set of candidate backup links among $V_a$, each $e_j$ carrying an activation energy $c_j>0$. A repair is a binary vector $\mathbf{x}\in\{0,1\}^{C}$ with $x_j=1$ activating $e_j$, producing the healed graph
\begin{equation}
G(\mathbf{x}) = \big(V_a,\; E_a \cup \{\,e_j : x_j=1\,\}\big),
\label{eq:healed}
\end{equation}
constrained by an energy budget $B$.

\subsection{Resilience Metrics}
Plain connectivity is too weak a target, since a tree spanning $V_a$ is connected yet shatters at the first bridge failure. We therefore optimize \emph{worst case survivability}, the fraction of surviving nodes in the largest two edge connected component $\mathrm{LTECC}(\cdot)$, the maximal subgraph with no bridge and hence the part guaranteed to remain connected after any single further link failure:
\begin{equation}
S(\mathbf{x}) = \big|\mathrm{LTECC}(G(\mathbf{x}))\big| \,/\, |V_a| \in [0,1].
\label{eq:surv}
\end{equation}
$\mathrm{LTECC}$ is computed by finding bridges with a linear time depth first traversal, deleting them, and taking the largest remaining component. As a smooth complement we use spectral robustness. With degree matrix $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{x})$ and adjacency $\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{x})$ of $G(\mathbf{x})$ over $V_a$, the Laplacian is $\mathbf{L}(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{x})-\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{x})$, and the algebraic connectivity is its second smallest eigenvalue, expressed variationally as
\begin{equation}
\lambda_2(\mathbf{x}) = \min_{\substack{\mathbf{v}\neq\mathbf{0},\; \mathbf{v}\perp\mathbf{1}}} \frac{\mathbf{v}^{\top}\mathbf{L}(\mathbf{x})\,\mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{v}^{\top}\mathbf{v}}.
\label{eq:lambda2}
\end{equation}
$\lambda_2(\mathbf{x})>0$ iff $G(\mathbf{x})$ is connected, and it increases with the number of edge disjoint paths, so it rewards redundancy and graceful degradation.

\subsection{Optimization Problem}
The repair problem maximizes a composite resilience score under the budget:
\begin{equation}
\max_{\mathbf{x}\in\{0,1\}^{C}} \; R(\mathbf{x}) = S(\mathbf{x}) + w\,\lambda_2(\mathbf{x})
\quad \text{s.t.} \quad \textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{C} c_j x_j \le B ,
\label{eq:opt}
\end{equation}
with weight $w\ge 0$. Problem~\eqref{eq:opt} is NP-hard and non submodular: enlarging the two edge connected core usually requires several jointly activated links that close a cycle, so the marginal value of any single link can be zero, and greedy per energy selection stalls, which motivates global search. To let the solver explore freely while still honoring the budget, we fold the constraint into a penalized fitness with a large coefficient $\rho$:
\begin{equation}
F(\mathbf{x}) = R(\mathbf{x}) - \rho\,\frac{\max\!\big(0,\; \sum_j c_j x_j - B\big)}{B},
\label{eq:fitness}
\end{equation}
so any over budget solution is pushed strictly below every feasible one.

\subsection{Quantum-Inspired Solver with AI Guidance}
Each decision variable is a qubit whose state is summarized by the probability $p_j=\Pr(x_j=1)=\sin^2\theta_j$, with $\theta_j$ a rotation angle. A population of probabilistic individuals is \emph{observed} (measured) each generation by sampling
\begin{equation}
x_j = \mathbb{1}\!\left[\,u_j < p_j\,\right], \qquad u_j \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1),
\label{eq:measure}
\end{equation}
scoring each sample by $F$ in \eqref{eq:fitness}, and tracking the best incumbent $\mathbf{b}$ seen so far. Every qubit is then rotated toward the incumbent and, simultaneously, toward the AI risk map $\mathbf{r}\in[0,1]^{C}$:
\begin{equation}
p_j \leftarrow \Pi_{[p_{\min},p_{\max}]}\!\Big(p_j + \eta\,(b_j - p_j) + \gamma\,(r_j - p_j)\Big),
\label{eq:rotation}
\end{equation}
where $\Pi$ clips to $[p_{\min},p_{\max}]$ to preserve exploration, $\eta$ is the rotation rate, and $\gamma$ is the prior pull. The population is initialized at the warm-start
\begin{equation}
p_j^{(0)} = r_j .
\label{eq:warmstart}
\end{equation}
Equations \eqref{eq:warmstart} and the $\gamma$ term of \eqref{eq:rotation} are the two halves of the forward synergy channel: $\mathbf{r}$ seeds the search in promising regions and then persistently prunes it toward them. The risk map itself is produced by the perception layer, $r_j=\phi(e_j; G_a)$, a per candidate score; our transparent surrogate sets $\phi$ high when $e_j$ bridges two surviving fragments and when its endpoints are thinly connected, both predictive of resilience leverage, and a learned predictor replaces $\phi$ in deployment. A classical genetic algorithm has no equally natural slot for $\mathbf{r}$; the qubit probability $p_j$ is that slot. With population $P$ over $T$ generations, the solver performs $PT$ evaluations of \eqref{eq:fitness}; since each evaluation costs an $O(|V_a|+|E|)$ bridge search and an $O(|V_a|^3)$ eigensolve for \eqref{eq:lambda2}, the resilience evaluation, not the search, dominates and is the scaling bottleneck. Two properties make this engine practical for IoT. It is \emph{anytime}: because the population always holds a best feasible incumbent, the controller can be interrupted at any generation and still actuate a usable configuration, which matters when a deadline is set by how fast the network is degrading. It is also naturally \emph{parallel and batchable}: a whole population is evaluated independently, so the per generation cost is dominated not by the optimizer but by the resilience evaluation, computing the survivable core and the algebraic connectivity for each candidate. That evaluation, rather than the search, is the scaling bottleneck, and it is amenable to batched linear algebra on accelerators, a point we return to among the open challenges.

Operationally, one healing cycle proceeds as follows. Telemetry reveals that nodes or links have failed; the perception layer localizes the damage and emits the risk map $\mathbf{r}$; the optimizer is warm-started from $\mathbf{r}$ and run for a bounded number of generations or until a deadline, since it is anytime; the chosen configuration is actuated by activating the selected backup links; and the realized outcome is logged. The detector in our study is a transparent surrogate that scores a candidate link by whether it bridges two surviving fragments and how thinly connected its endpoints are, both strong predictors of resilience leverage, which keeps the mechanism reproducible and auditable. In deployment this surrogate is replaced by a small learned predictor. The return channel closes the loop: configurations that proved resilient become labelled experience, so the detector stays calibrated as topology and demand drift, and the prior it supplies to the optimizer improves over time rather than degrading. This feedback is what makes the system a loop rather than a pipeline, and it is why we treat the detector and the optimizer as co-designed rather than stacked.

\subsection{CS1: Reconfiguration under Cascading Failures}
We generate $200$ node random geometric meshes, remove $32\%$ of nodes by a targeted degree based cascade, and set the repair budget to $3.5\%$ of the total candidate link energy, a deliberately tight budget under which full reconnection is not free. The controller activates backup links to maximize the worst case survivability of \eqref{eq:surv}. We compare it against a naive greedy that adds the backup link with the best marginal survivability per unit energy until no link helps, a stronger connectivity first greedy, and two strong metaheuristics, a genetic algorithm and simulated annealing, all sharing the optimizer's evaluation budget. Figure~\ref{fig:cs1} reports the outcome. The naive greedy controller heals to only $0.91\pm0.12$ survivable fraction and, tellingly, spends barely a tenth of the available energy before it stalls: once the network is merely connected, no single further link improves worst case survivability, so it sees nothing worth buying and leaves both the budget and the redundancy on the table (Wilcoxon $p=2\times10^{-4}$ against the AI guided optimizer). The population based methods use the budget and heal far better: the genetic algorithm and the connectivity first greedy reach $\approx 1.00$, the quantum-inspired optimizer with AI guidance reaches $0.98\pm0.03$, and simulated annealing $0.97\pm0.05$. We state the ranking plainly. The genetic algorithm and the connectivity first greedy are strongest here; the quantum-inspired optimizer is competitive, beating simulated annealing ($p=0.06$) while trailing the genetic search. Its role is not to dominate the solver portfolio but to be the substrate into which AI guidance fuses, which is what we isolate next.

The synergy shows itself most clearly when we isolate the AI warm-start (Figure~\ref{fig:synergy}). Here in CS1 its main effect is on reliability and speed rather than on the average. The mean survivable fraction improves only modestly, from $0.972$ without guidance to $0.980$ with it, a gain within the run to run noise at this scale ($p=0.17$), but the variability of the outcome is halved, from a standard deviation of $0.063$ to $0.031$, and healing reaches ninety five percent of its final quality in fewer generations ($81$ versus $88$). Without guidance the optimizer occasionally converges to a poor configuration; with guidance it is dependably good. For resilience, where a dependable and prompt recovery matters as much as an average one, this tail narrowing is the point: an operator cannot trust a controller that is excellent on average but now and then leaves the network fragile after a fault. That the warm-start lifts the average significantly as well, we show on the harder CS2 instances in the next subsection, and the mechanism behind both effects we isolate in the ablation of Section~\ref{sec:ablation}.

\subsection{CS2: Restoring Gateway Reachability under Link Jamming}
The second study changes both the threat and the objective. A $220$ node IoT mesh reports to two gateways. An adversary jams a geographic region, a disk in which every link is knocked out at once, removing about a quarter of the links in a contiguous block rather than at random. The resilience target is now sink oriented: the controller activates backup links, under an energy budget, to maximize the fraction of devices that retain two edge disjoint paths to a gateway, that is, that share a two edge connected component with a gateway and so stay reachable after any single further link failure. After jamming only about half of the devices meet this bar.

Unlike a separable reallocation, this is again a combinatorial, non submodular repair, so it is a genuine second test of the same framework rather than a restatement of CS1. The results, in Figure~\ref{fig:cs2}, follow the CS1 pattern but with a sharper synergy. The naive greedy again fails, restoring only $0.74\pm0.19$ and stalling after a handful of links while leaving most of the budget unspent ($p<10^{-14}$ against the AI guided optimizer). The connectivity first greedy and the genetic algorithm lead ($1.00\pm0.01$ and $0.92\pm0.04$), and the quantum-inspired optimizer is again competitive, reaching $0.89\pm0.05$ and significantly beating simulated annealing at $0.87\pm0.04$ ($p=9\times10^{-4}$). Crucially, here the AI warm-start delivers a large and highly significant average gain, lifting the optimizer from $0.84\pm0.05$ without guidance to $0.89\pm0.05$ with it ($p=4\times10^{-8}$). Where CS1 showed the synergy as a reliability gain, CS2 shows it as a decisive quality gain, and on a different threat model and a different resilience objective. Across the two studies the message is consistent: reactive myopic repair leaves IoT networks fragile under both random cascades and targeted jamming, global optimization restores them, and fusing AI guidance into the quantum-inspired optimizer makes the healing both more reliable and, on the harder instances, significantly better.

\section{What Drives the Synergy: An Ablation}\label{sec:ablation}

We isolate each design choice on CS1; Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} and Table~\ref{tab:ablation} report the results.

\noindent\textbf{Synergy components.} This is the most informative ablation. Seeding the optimizer with the AI risk map but then letting it drift, that is, warm-start with $\gamma=0$, barely helps: survivability rises only from $0.892\pm0.132$ without any guidance to $0.902\pm0.128$, and both remain highly variable. The decisive ingredient is the persistent pruning pull: with the full mechanism, warm-start together with the $\gamma$ term in \eqref{eq:rotation}, survivability jumps to $0.978\pm0.026$ and the standard deviation collapses about fivefold. The lesson is specific: it is not enough to point the optimizer at the right region once; the AI prior must keep pruning the search throughout. The synergy is a sustained coupling, and its main payoff is reliability.

\noindent\textbf{Robustness weight $w$.} Setting $w=0$ in \eqref{eq:opt} optimizes survivability alone and yields the lowest $\lambda_2$ (least redundancy); increasing $w$ trades a little survivability for markedly higher spectral robustness, identifying a balanced operating point.

\noindent\textbf{Energy budget.} Sweeping the budget traces the resilience versus energy curve: survivability rises steeply then saturates, revealing the budget beyond which extra energy buys little additional resilience, a directly actionable provisioning guide.

\section{Challenges and Future Directions}\label{sec:challenges}

\noindent\textbf{Scaling the spectral objective.} The healing bottleneck is graph analysis, computing $\lambda_2$ and the survivable core, rather than the optimizer. Batched spectral evaluation on accelerators, and incremental updates as links are toggled, are the path to city scale meshes.

\noindent\textbf{Distributed and federated healing.} The loop here is centralized. Real deployments call for gateways that cooperate to heal local regions without a global controller, which connects naturally to federated learning for the perception layer.

\noindent\textbf{Securing the loop.} The closed loop is itself an attack surface. A compromised or spoofed detector could steer the optimizer toward fragile configurations, so the perception to optimization channel needs integrity guarantees and anomaly checks on the risk map.

\noindent\textbf{Toward true quantum optimization.} The quantum-inspired layer is a stepping stone. As small quantum processors mature, the same warm-start prior could seed a variational or annealing based quantum optimizer, and our probabilistic formulation is meant to make that transition direct.

\noindent\textbf{Richer perception.} Our detector is a transparent surrogate. Learned spatiotemporal predictors of cascades and jamming, trained partly on the loop's own recovery experience, are a clear next step that the return channel already supports.

\noindent\textbf{Limitations.} We are explicit about the study's scope. The evaluation is simulation based, on random geometric topologies and synthetic fault and jamming models rather than traces from a deployed testbed, so absolute numbers should be read as relative comparisons under controlled conditions. The perception layer is a transparent surrogate rather than a trained model, which isolates the synergy mechanism cleanly but understates what a learned detector could contribute. The optimizer is quantum-inspired and classical, so we claim no quantum advantage, only that the formulation is ready for one. Finally, our worst case survivability target considers single link failures; multi failure and correlated attack models, and a true min over adversarial removals, are natural and harder extensions. None of these caveats changes the paper's central, repeatedly tested finding, that a sustained AI to optimizer coupling restores resilience more reliably than reactive repair, but they bound how far the specific numbers should be carried.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a quantum-AI closed-loop framework for self-healing IoT networks, cast repair as budget constrained survivability maximization, and showed across two distinct threats, random cascading failures and targeted link jamming, that an AI risk map fused into a quantum-inspired optimizer restores resilience that myopic repair cannot. The fusion makes healing more reliable and faster on the cascading-failure instances and significantly better on the harder jamming instances, while the optimizer honestly remains competitive with, rather than superior to, a strong genetic search. An ablation pinpoints the mechanism: it is the sustained pull of the AI prior on the probabilistic qubit state, not a one shot warm-start, that drives the gain. We see the framework as a practical and honest path toward IoT networks that heal themselves, and as a bridge to the quantum optimizers to come.
