\begin{abstract}
Internet of Things deployments fragment under cascading device failures and targeted jamming, and the damage is rarely a single clean break: a network that is merely reconnected after a fault often remains one link away from the next collapse. We argue that resilience asks for a closed loop rather than a one shot repair, and we present a self-healing framework that couples a lightweight artificial intelligence perception layer, which detects and localizes degradation, with a quantum-inspired optimization layer, which recomputes a resilient configuration under an energy budget. The synergy is concrete: the perception layer produces a risk map that warm-starts and prunes the quantum-inspired search through its probabilistic qubit representation, and recovered configurations are fed back to keep the detector calibrated. Across two case studies, restoring worst case survivability after cascading failures and reallocating resources under a jamming surge, the loop restores resilience that myopic repair cannot, and the warm start makes healing both faster and markedly more reliable. We report the comparison honestly: the quantum-inspired optimizer is competitive with strong metaheuristics rather than dominant, and its value lies in being the natural substrate for fusing learned guidance into combinatorial healing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Internet of Things now carries traffic that critical infrastructure depends on, yet the networks that carry it are fragile. Battery exhaustion, weather, and physical tampering remove nodes in bursts, and a single hub loss can cascade as newly isolated neighbours drop in turn. Adversaries add a sharper threat: a jammer can silence a band of channels at the very moment demand spikes. The shared property of these events is not that they disconnect a network once, but that they leave it brittle, sitting on single points of failure where the next small disturbance becomes the next outage.

Classical recovery tends to be reactive and myopic. A controller that reconnects the largest possible component, or that assigns each device to its cheapest free channel, optimizes the situation in front of it and stops. Such repair is fast, but it spends little thought on the failure after this one, so the healed network is connected without being survivable. Making a network survivable, meaning it stays connected after any further single failure, is a global and combinatorial property: it usually requires several coordinated link activations that close redundant cycles, where no single activation looks worthwhile on its own. This is precisely where a greedy controller stalls.

Two technologies are maturing that, taken together, address this gap. Artificial intelligence at the edge can detect and localize degradation in milliseconds from streaming telemetry. Quantum-inspired optimization brings population based, anytime, and highly parallel global search to the combinatorial reconfiguration problem, without requiring a quantum processor. Our thesis is that their synergy, not either one alone, is what enables genuine self-healing: the learned risk map is the right object to steer a global optimizer, and the optimizer is the right engine to turn that map into a resilient configuration.

We make four contributions. First, we formulate self-healing as a closed loop that senses, detects, reoptimizes, actuates, and learns. Second, we identify a concrete synergy mechanism: the perception layer warms up and prunes the quantum-inspired search through its probabilistic qubit amplitudes, and recovered configurations retrain the detector. Third, we evaluate the loop on two distinct resilience threats. Fourth, we report an honest portfolio comparison, including strong classical baselines, and locate the quantum-inspired optimizer's value in fusion and reliability rather than raw dominance.

\section{Why a Quantum-AI Synergy for Resilience}

Resilience in IoT spans several coupled challenges: topological robustness against node and link loss, robustness of wireless access against interference and jamming, and all of it under tight energy budgets. No single technique answers all of them, which is why a framework that combines complementary strengths is attractive.

Artificial intelligence contributes speed and anticipation. A small model running at a gateway or on a device can flag which nodes are becoming critical, predict where a cascade will propagate, and estimate post fault demand, all far faster than any exhaustive search. What it does not provide is a guarantee that its decisions compose into a globally resilient configuration.

Quantum-inspired optimization contributes global combinatorial search. Quantum-inspired evolutionary algorithms represent each decision variable as a qubit whose state is a probability, evolve a population of such probabilistic individuals, and rotate them toward the best solutions observed. They run on commodity hardware, scale to large IoT instances, are naturally parallel, and are anytime, returning a usable configuration whenever the deadline arrives. What they lack is any sense of where in a vast search space the good solutions lie.

The synergy follows from these complementary gaps, and it is more than co-location. The perception layer outputs exactly the prior that the optimizer needs, and the optimizer's probabilistic substrate is exactly the place to absorb it: a learned per element risk score maps directly onto the initial qubit amplitudes, warm-starting the population in promising regions and gently pruning the rest. A purely classical genetic algorithm has no equally natural slot for such a prior. \section{The Self-Healing Closed Loop}

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} shows the framework. Telemetry from the network feeds a perception layer that detects and localizes degradation and emits a risk map over network elements. The optimization layer takes the degraded state and the risk map and recomputes a configuration, which set of backup links to activate, or which device to channel assignments to adopt, that maximizes a resilience objective under an energy or power budget. An actuation layer applies the configuration, and the outcome is fed back to keep the detector calibrated as conditions drift.

Two channels carry the synergy. In the forward channel, the risk map biases the optimizer's initial qubit amplitudes toward elements the detector flags as high leverage, and applies a small persistent pull toward that prior throughout the search, which both warm-starts and prunes. In the return channel, configurations that proved resilient become labelled experience that keeps the lightweight detector accurate under non-stationary conditions. The framework is solver agnostic, but the forward channel is most natural for the quantum-inspired optimizer, whose decision variables already are probabilities.

\section{Case Study 1: Reconfiguration under Cascading Failures}

We model an IoT mesh of 200 nodes placed at random in the plane, connected when within communication range. A targeted attack removes the highest degree nodes one by one, and each removal triggers a cascade as nodes that lose all neighbours also drop. After the attack roughly one third of the nodes are gone and the survivors fragment, with the largest connected component holding under two thirds of them. The controller may activate backup links from a pre surveyed set, each with an energy cost, under a tight budget equal to a small fraction of the cost of activating all of them.

The objective is worst case survivability, the fraction of surviving nodes that lie in the largest two edge connected component, the part of the network that stays connected after any single further link failure. We add a small reward for spectral robustness, measured by algebraic connectivity, to break ties toward globally well knit solutions. The quantum-inspired optimizer selects which backup links to activate; the AI risk map favours links that bridge fragments and reinforce thinly connected regions. We compare against a naive greedy controller that adds links by marginal resilience per energy, a stronger connectivity first greedy, and a genetic algorithm, all over thirty random scenarios.

Figure~\ref{fig:cs1} reports the outcome. The naive greedy controller heals to only $0.90 \pm 0.13$ survivable fraction and, tellingly, spends barely a quarter of the available energy before it stalls: once the network is merely connected, no single further link improves worst case survivability, so the greedy rule sees nothing worth buying and leaves the budget and the network's redundancy on the table. The population based methods use the budget and heal far better. The genetic algorithm reaches $0.998 \pm 0.005$ and the quantum-inspired optimizer with AI guidance reaches $0.979 \pm 0.026$, both significantly above naive greedy. The connectivity first greedy is a strong baseline at $0.983 \pm 0.017$. We state plainly that the genetic algorithm is the strongest solver here; the quantum-inspired optimizer is competitive with the strong greedy, not dominant.

The synergy appears most clearly when we isolate the AI warm-start. Figure~\ref{fig:synergy} compares the quantum-inspired optimizer with and without it. The warm start improves the mean survivable fraction, but its larger effect is on reliability: it cuts the variability of the healed outcome roughly threefold, from a standard deviation of $0.079$ down to $0.026$. Without guidance the optimizer occasionally converges to a poor configuration; with guidance it is dependably good. It also reaches ninety five percent of its final quality in fewer generations, so healing completes sooner. For resilience, where a dependable and prompt recovery matters as much as an average one, this reliability gain is the point.

\section{Case Study 2: Reallocation under a Jamming Surge}

The second study changes the threat. A set of 120 IoT devices reach gateways over sixteen wireless channels. An adversary jams about a third of the channels, zeroing their usable rate, at the same instant a demand surge triples the traffic of roughly a third of the devices. The controller reassigns devices to surviving channels to maximize served demand under a transmit power budget, with each channel having finite capacity and each device using at most one channel.

This problem is structurally different from CS1. It is capacity bound and close to separable, so a congestion aware greedy heuristic, which assigns devices in value per power order while respecting capacity, is near optimal. Over thirty scenarios the greedy controller serves $0.65 \pm 0.02$ of the surged demand, the genetic algorithm $0.56 \pm 0.03$, and the quantum-inspired optimizer $0.48 \pm 0.02$. Here the metaheuristics do not help and the AI warm-start gives no significant gain. Figure~\ref{fig:cs2} shows the comparison.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../results/cs2/fig_cs2.png}
\caption{CS2. Served demand fraction under a jamming surge. On this capacity bound reallocation a congestion aware greedy controller within the framework is near optimal, so the framework selects it as the in loop solver.}
\label{fig:cs2}
\end{figure}

We read this honestly and constructively. The framework is solver agnostic, and CS2 shows why that matters: not every resilience problem needs heavy combinatorial search. On a convex, capacity bound reallocation, the right engineering choice is the lightweight greedy controller, and the AI's contribution is detection, identifying the jammed channels and surged devices that drive the reallocation. The lesson across the two studies is that the loop adapts its optimizer to the hardness of the problem: global quantum-inspired search where survivability is combinatorial, as in CS1, and a cheap heuristic where reallocation is near separable, as in CS2.

\section{Discussion and Open Challenges}

The two studies together support a measured claim. A closed loop that pairs learned perception with budget aware optimization restores resilience that reactive repair leaves on the table, and the learned guidance, fused through the quantum-inspired optimizer's probabilistic substrate, makes combinatorial healing both faster and more reliable. We deliberately avoid an overclaim: the quantum-inspired optimizer is not a universal winner, and a genetic algorithm matched or beat it on quality. Its distinctive value is being the natural carrier for AI guidance and offering anytime, parallel, budget aware search, all on commodity hardware.

Several challenges remain. Scaling the survivability computation to very large meshes calls for batched spectral evaluation on accelerators, since the bottleneck is graph analysis rather than the optimizer itself. Distributed and federated healing, where gateways cooperate without a central controller, fits IoT deployments better than the centralized loop studied here. The loop is also an attack surface: a compromised detector could mislead the optimizer, so securing the perception to optimization channel matters. Finally, the quantum-inspired layer is a stepping stone. As small quantum processors mature, the same warm-start prior could seed a true quantum optimizer, and our probabilistic formulation is meant to make that transition direct.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a quantum-AI closed-loop framework for self-healing IoT networks, in which an AI perception layer steers a quantum-inspired optimizer toward resilient configurations and recovered configurations retrain the detector. On cascading failures the loop restores worst case survivability that myopic repair cannot, and the AI warm-start makes healing markedly more reliable; on a jamming surge the same framework adapts its solver to a near separable problem. The synergy is real and useful, and reported without exaggeration. We see the framework as a practical and honest path toward IoT networks that heal themselves, and as a bridge to the quantum optimizers to come.
