\title{Self-Healing IoT Networks: A Quantum-AI Closed-Loop Framework for Resilience}

\author{Do Phuc Hao and collaborators%
\thanks{D. P. Hao is with the Center for AI Innovation, Research, and Application (CAIRA), Da Nang Architecture University, Da Nang, Vietnam (e-mail: haodp.sut@gmail.com).}%

\begin{abstract}
Internet of Things deployments fragment under cascading device failures and targeted jamming, and a network that is merely reconnected after a fault often remains one link away from the next collapse. We argue that resilience asks for a closed loop rather than a one shot repair, and we present a self-healing framework that couples a lightweight artificial intelligence perception layer, which detects and localizes degradation, with a quantum-inspired optimization layer, which recomputes a resilient configuration under an energy budget. The synergy is concrete: the perception layer produces a risk map that warm-starts and prunes the quantum-inspired search through its probabilistic qubit representation, and recovered configurations are fed back to keep the detector calibrated. We cast repair as a budget constrained maximization of a worst case survivability objective augmented with spectral robustness, and we evaluate the loop on two distinct threats across thirty random scenarios each. On cascading failures the loop restores survivability that myopic repair cannot, and an ablation shows the warm-start makes healing both faster and about three times more reliable. On a jamming surge the same framework adapts its solver to a near separable problem. We report the comparison honestly: the quantum-inspired optimizer is competitive with strong metaheuristics rather than dominant, and its value is in being the natural substrate for fusing learned guidance into combinatorial healing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} Internet of Things now carries traffic that critical infrastructure depends on, yet the networks that carry it are fragile. Battery exhaustion, weather, and physical tampering remove nodes in bursts, and a single hub loss can cascade as newly isolated neighbours drop in turn. Adversaries add a sharper threat: a jammer can silence a band of channels at the very moment demand spikes. The shared property of these events is not that they disconnect a network once, but that they leave it brittle, sitting on single points of failure where the next small disturbance becomes the next outage.

Classical recovery tends to be reactive and myopic. A controller that reconnects the largest possible component, or that assigns each device to its cheapest free channel, optimizes the situation in front of it and stops. Such repair is fast, but it spends little thought on the failure after this one, so the healed network is connected without being survivable. Making a network survivable, meaning it stays connected after any further single failure, is a global and combinatorial property: it usually requires several coordinated link activations that close redundant cycles, where no single activation looks worthwhile on its own. This is precisely where a greedy controller stalls, a behaviour we both observe and dissect by ablation.

Two technologies are maturing that, together, address this gap. Artificial intelligence at the edge can detect and localize degradation in milliseconds from streaming telemetry. Quantum-inspired optimization brings population based, anytime, and highly parallel global search to the combinatorial reconfiguration problem, without requiring a quantum processor. Our thesis is that their synergy, not either one alone, enables genuine self-healing: the learned risk map is the right object to steer a global optimizer, and the optimizer is the right engine to turn that map into a resilient configuration.

We make four contributions. First, we formalize self-healing as a closed loop and cast repair as budget constrained survivability maximization. Second, we identify and instrument a concrete synergy mechanism: the perception layer warm-starts and prunes the quantum-inspired search through its qubit amplitudes, and recovered configurations retrain the detector. Third, we evaluate the loop on two distinct resilience threats with multi seed statistics. Fourth, we provide an ablation that isolates the warm-start, the search-pruning pull, the robustness weight, and the energy budget, and we report an honest portfolio comparison that locates the quantum-inspired optimizer's value in fusion and reliability rather than raw dominance.

\section{Why a Quantum-AI Synergy for Resilience}\label{sec:why}

Resilience in IoT spans several coupled challenges: topological robustness against node and link loss, robustness of wireless access against interference and jamming, and all of it under tight energy budgets. No single technique answers all of them, which is why a framework that combines complementary strengths is attractive.

Artificial intelligence contributes speed and anticipation. A small model running at a gateway or on a device can flag which nodes are becoming critical, predict where a cascade will propagate, and estimate post fault demand, all far faster than any exhaustive search. What it does not provide is a guarantee that its decisions compose into a globally resilient configuration.

Quantum-inspired optimization contributes global combinatorial search. Quantum-inspired evolutionary algorithms represent each decision variable as a qubit whose state is a probability, evolve a population of such probabilistic individuals, and rotate them toward the best solutions observed. They run on commodity hardware, scale to large IoT instances, are naturally parallel, and are anytime, returning a usable configuration whenever the deadline arrives. What they lack is any sense of where in a vast search space the good solutions lie.

Interest in marrying these technologies for networks is rising quickly. Recent work has studied quantum federated learning over classical and quantum networks and its use in space air ground integrated systems, quantum machine learning as a primitive for communication intelligence, and quantum and quantum-inspired optimization for routing, scheduling, and resource allocation. Most of this effort targets either learning or optimization in isolation, and treats faults as a backdrop rather than the object of study. Network resilience, and self-healing in particular, has been examined largely through classical survivability design and reactive restoration. The gap we address sits exactly between these threads: using learned perception to drive quantum-inspired combinatorial search, in a loop whose explicit purpose is to keep an IoT network resilient as it is damaged and repaired in real time.

The synergy follows from these complementary gaps, and it is more than co-location. The perception layer outputs exactly the prior that the optimizer needs, and the optimizer's probabilistic substrate is exactly the place to absorb it: a learned per element risk score maps directly onto the initial qubit amplitudes, warm-starting the population in promising regions and gently pruning the rest. A purely classical genetic algorithm has no equally natural slot for such a prior. \subsection{What to Optimize: A Resilience Objective}
We model the IoT deployment as a graph $G=(V,E)$. A fault removes nodes, leaving a fragmented residual graph over the surviving nodes $V_a$. A controller activates backup links from a candidate set, encoded by a binary vector $\mathbf{x}$, where $x_j=1$ activates candidate $j$ at energy cost $c_j$, under a budget $B$. Connectivity alone is too weak a target, so we optimize worst case survivability, the fraction of surviving nodes in the largest two edge connected component $\mathrm{LTECC}$, the part that stays connected after any single further link failure:
\begin{equation}
S(\mathbf{x}) = \big|\mathrm{LTECC}(G(\mathbf{x}))\big| \,/\, |V_a|.
\label{eq:surv}
\end{equation}
We add spectral robustness through the algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2(\mathbf{x})$, the second smallest Laplacian eigenvalue, which is positive only when the graph is connected and grows with edge disjoint redundancy. The repair problem is then
\begin{equation}
\max_{\mathbf{x}\in\{0,1\}^{C}} \; S(\mathbf{x}) + w\,\lambda_2(\mathbf{x})
\quad \text{s.t.} \quad \textstyle\sum_j c_j x_j \le B .
\label{eq:opt}
\end{equation}
Problem~\eqref{eq:opt} is NP-hard and non submodular: enlarging the two edge connected core usually needs several jointly activated links that close a cycle, so the marginal value of any single link can be zero. Greedy per energy selection therefore stalls, which motivates global search.

\subsection{How to Optimize: Quantum-Inspired Search with AI Guidance}
Each decision variable is a qubit summarized by $p_j=\Pr(x_j=1)$. A population is sampled, $x_j\sim\mathrm{Bernoulli}(p_j)$, scored by \eqref{eq:opt} with a hard penalty for budget violation, and the best incumbent $\mathbf{b}$ is tracked. Each qubit is rotated toward the incumbent and, simultaneously, toward the AI risk map $\mathbf{r}$:
\begin{equation}
p_j \leftarrow p_j + \eta\,(b_j - p_j) + \gamma\,(r_j - p_j),
\label{eq:rotation}
\end{equation}
with the population initialized at the warm-start $p_j^{(0)} = r_j$. Equations \eqref{eq:rotation} and the warm-start are the two halves of the forward synergy channel: $\mathbf{r}$ seeds the search in promising regions and the $\gamma$ term persistently prunes toward them. A classical genetic algorithm has no equally natural slot for $\mathbf{r}$; the qubit probability is that slot.

Operationally, one healing cycle proceeds as follows. Telemetry reveals that nodes or links have failed; the perception layer localizes the damage and emits the risk map $\mathbf{r}$; the optimizer is warm-started from $\mathbf{r}$ and run for a bounded number of generations or until a deadline, since it is anytime; the chosen configuration is actuated by activating the selected backup links or reassigning channels; and the realized outcome is logged. The detector in our study is a transparent surrogate that scores a candidate link by whether it bridges two surviving fragments and how thinly connected its endpoints are, both strong predictors of resilience leverage, which keeps the mechanism reproducible and auditable. In deployment this surrogate is replaced by a small learned predictor. The return channel closes the loop: configurations that proved resilient become labelled experience, so the detector stays calibrated as topology and demand drift, and the prior it supplies to the optimizer improves over time rather than degrading. This feedback is what makes the system a loop rather than a pipeline, and it is why we treat the detector and the optimizer as co-designed rather than stacked.

\subsection{CS1: Reconfiguration under Cascading Failures}
We generate $200$ node random geometric meshes, remove $32\%$ of nodes by targeted cascade, and set the repair budget to $3.5\%$ of the total candidate link energy, a deliberately tight budget under which full reconnection is not free. We compare four controllers within the same framework: a naive greedy that adds the backup link with the best marginal resilience per unit energy until no link helps, a stronger connectivity first greedy that first maximizes plain connectivity and only then redundancy, a genetic algorithm given the same evaluation budget as the quantum-inspired optimizer, and the quantum-inspired optimizer with and without the AI warm-start. The two greedy controllers represent the reactive repair common in practice, and the connectivity first variant is included specifically so that our comparison is not against a straw baseline. Figure~\ref{fig:cs1} reports the outcome. The naive greedy controller heals to only $0.90\pm0.13$ survivable fraction and, tellingly, spends barely a quarter of the available energy before it stalls: once the network is merely connected, no single further link improves worst case survivability, so it sees nothing worth buying and leaves the budget and the redundancy on the table. The population based methods use the budget and heal far better. The genetic algorithm reaches $0.998\pm0.005$ and the quantum-inspired optimizer with AI guidance reaches $0.979\pm0.026$, both significantly above naive greedy (Wilcoxon $p=0.015$). We state plainly that the genetic algorithm is the strongest solver here; the quantum-inspired optimizer is competitive with the strong connectivity greedy, not dominant.

The synergy is clearest when we isolate the AI warm-start (Figure~\ref{fig:synergy}). The warm start improves the mean survivable fraction, but its larger effect is reliability: it cuts the variability of the healed outcome about threefold, from a standard deviation of $0.079$ to $0.026$. Without guidance the optimizer occasionally converges to a poor configuration; with guidance it is dependably good, and it reaches ninety five percent of its final quality in fewer generations. For resilience, where a dependable and prompt recovery matters as much as an average one, this reliability gain is the point.

\subsection{CS2: Reallocation under a Jamming Surge}
The second study changes the threat. A set of $120$ IoT devices reach gateways over sixteen channels, each channel offering a rate and costing a transmit power that depend on distance, and each channel having a finite capacity. An adversary jams about a third of the channels, zeroing their usable rate, while a surge triples the demand of about a third of the devices, so the network must simultaneously route around dead channels and absorb a load spike. The controller reassigns devices to surviving channels to maximize served demand under a transmit power budget, with each device using at most one channel.

This problem is structurally unlike CS1. It is capacity bound and close to separable: serving a device is largely an independent decision, the binding constraints are simple, and a good assignment can be built element by element. A congestion aware greedy that admits devices in decreasing value per power, respecting capacity and budget, is therefore near optimal, serving $0.65\pm0.02$ of surged demand, above the genetic algorithm ($0.56\pm0.03$) and the quantum-inspired optimizer ($0.48\pm0.02$); see Figure~\ref{fig:cs2}. Here the population based search and the AI warm-start give no significant advantage. We read this constructively rather than as a negative result. The framework is solver agnostic, and CS2 shows that not every resilience problem needs heavy combinatorial search: when the repair decomposes, a lightweight greedy controller inside the loop is both near optimal and cheap, and the AI's contribution shifts to detection, identifying the jammed channels and surged devices that define the reallocation. Taken together, the two studies show the loop matching its optimizer to the hardness of the threat, global quantum-inspired search where survivability is combinatorial and coupled, as in CS1, and a cheap heuristic where reallocation is near separable, as in CS2. The discipline of choosing the cheapest adequate solver, rather than always reaching for the heaviest one, is itself a contribution for resource constrained IoT.

\section{What Drives the Synergy: An Ablation}\label{sec:ablation}

We isolate each design choice on CS1; Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} and Table~\ref{tab:ablation} report the results.

\noindent\textbf{Synergy components.} Removing the AI warm-start (uniform prior) and removing the persistent pruning pull ($\gamma=0$) both degrade the outcome. Survivable fraction falls from $0.978$ with the full mechanism to $0.889$ without any AI guidance, and the standard deviation widens about threefold, confirming that the warm-start and the $\gamma$ term in \eqref{eq:rotation} are the operative synergy and that their main benefit is reliability.

\noindent\textbf{Robustness weight $w$.} Setting $w=0$ in \eqref{eq:opt} optimizes survivability alone and yields the lowest $\lambda_2$ (least redundancy); increasing $w$ trades a little survivability for markedly higher spectral robustness, identifying a balanced operating point.

\noindent\textbf{Energy budget.} Sweeping the budget traces the resilience versus energy curve: survivability rises steeply then saturates, revealing the budget beyond which extra energy buys little additional resilience, a directly actionable provisioning guide.

\section{Challenges and Future Directions}\label{sec:challenges}

\noindent\textbf{Scaling the spectral objective.} The healing bottleneck is graph analysis, computing $\lambda_2$ and the survivable core, rather than the optimizer. Batched spectral evaluation on accelerators, and incremental updates as links are toggled, are the path to city scale meshes.

\noindent\textbf{Distributed and federated healing.} The loop here is centralized. Real deployments call for gateways that cooperate to heal local regions without a global controller, which connects naturally to federated learning for the perception layer.

\noindent\textbf{Securing the loop.} The closed loop is itself an attack surface. A compromised or spoofed detector could steer the optimizer toward fragile configurations, so the perception to optimization channel needs integrity guarantees and anomaly checks on the risk map.

\noindent\textbf{Toward true quantum optimization.} The quantum-inspired layer is a stepping stone. As small quantum processors mature, the same warm-start prior could seed a variational or annealing based quantum optimizer, and our probabilistic formulation is meant to make that transition direct.

\noindent\textbf{Richer perception.} Our detector is a transparent surrogate. Learned spatiotemporal predictors of cascades and jamming, trained partly on the loop's own recovery experience, are a clear next step that the return channel already supports.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a quantum-AI closed-loop framework for self-healing IoT networks, cast repair as budget constrained survivability maximization, and showed that an AI risk map fused into a quantum-inspired optimizer restores resilience that myopic repair cannot, more reliably and faster, while honestly remaining competitive with rather than superior to strong metaheuristics. The framework adapts its solver to the hardness of each threat, combinatorial survivability after cascading failures and lightweight reallocation under jamming. We see it as a practical and honest path toward IoT networks that heal themselves, and as a bridge to the quantum optimizers to come.
